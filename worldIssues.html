The World is Experiencing Critical Issues with Access to STEM Education

STEM EDUCATION

STEM education globally faces several significant challenges, including limited access to quality resources and trained educators, especially in rural and underserved areas. There is often a lack of funding and infrastructure to support advanced science and technology programs. Additionally, socio-economic disparities contribute to unequal opportunities, with students from disadvantaged backgrounds having less exposure to STEM fields. Gender and cultural biases further exacerbate these issues, discouraging participation from girls and minority groups. Moreover, the rapid pace of technological advancements requires continuous curriculum updates, which many education systems struggle to keep up with, resulting in outdated teaching methods and materials.

Roadblocks in Access to STEM Education

Issues with STEM in Rural Communities

Rachel S. Harris' thesis addresses STEM education challenges in rural schools due to limited resources, inadequate funding, unqualified teachers, and cultural barriers. As a Georgia Southern University professor, Harris emphasizes the need to improve STEM education to meet global demands. Harris highlights the importance of STEM for economic growth, innovation, and job market preparation. Harris points out that outdated technology and insufficient STEM materials, along with less funding compared to urban and suburban schools, hinder STEM education in rural areas. Harris also discusses the difficulty in attracting qualified teachers due to lower salaries and fewer incentives. Cultural factors, such as lower expectations and support influenced by parents' educational backgrounds, further impede progress. Harris concludes that making STEM education relevant to local contexts can engage and support students and parents, stressing the urgency of resolving these issues to remain globally competitive and ensure equal education for all students.

Issues Teachers are Facing

Kelly C. Margot thesis argues that despite the importance of STEM education, teachers face numerous challenges such as pedagogical, curriculum, structural, student-related, and assessment issues. Margot’s goal is to emphasize the necessity of effective STEM education for preparing students for the growing demand for STEM-literate workers. Margot, a professor at Grand Valley State University with a Ph.D. in Educational Psychology, notes the shortage of individuals in the STEM job market and stresses the importance of overcoming these challenges. Margot identifies significant barriers, including pedagogical and curricular challenges, structural limitations, student capability concerns, and insufficient support and assessment tools. Margot suggests improvements like collaborative opportunities, enhanced curriculum, district support, and professional development. Margot concludes by highlighting the need for comprehensive professional development and systemic support to boost teachers' ability to deliver STEM education.

Systemic Issues in STEM Education

Jeremi S. London's thesis states that systemic barriers, including racism, classism, and limited STEM education access, are hindering Black children's participation and success in STEM. As a Virginia Tech Engineering Education professor, London aims to help educators and researchers address these barriers for an inclusive STEM workforce. London’s review of 41 articles identifies socioeconomic factors, education quality, community contexts, and local support as key issues. London argues that systemic racism and classism limit Black children's STEM access and that social perceptions and limited activities in Black communities dampen interest. London emphasizes the importance of culturally responsive teaching and family support in fostering STEM aspirations. London concludes that early STEM programming, relevant pedagogy, teacher development, and community engagement are crucial for enhancing Black Americans' role in engineering.
